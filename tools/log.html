Before Every Test — Always
Rinse all tubes with pool water twice before starting, twice between each test, and twice at the end.
Hold dropper bottles completely vertical when dispensing to ensure consistent drop size.

Read colors in natural daylight. Hold the comparator against a white background for best accuracy.

Two readings, one tube. Do not dump between Free and Combined Chlorine. Using a 25 mL sample: each drop of R-0871 = 0.2 ppm.

Why it matters: Free chlorine is the active disinfectant. Combined chlorine is spent chlorine that provides no sanitization. Both need to be logged every test.

Parameters, Targets & Who Manages What
Water chemistry is managed in three layers: your daily adjustments, the automatic IPS doser, and Lucid Pool & Spa's weekly and monthly visits. Your job is the front line — test, read, and apply what the Log tab recommends.
ParameterTargetManaged ByWhat to Do
LSI
A calculated index of overall water balance — accounts for pH, calcium, alkalinity, temperature, and TDS. The single best indicator of whether water is balanced, scaling, or corrosive. Requires CH and TDS from Lucid's weekly readings to calculate.
 0.0 ±0.2

High (> +0.3): lower pH slightly with muriatic acid. If persistent, flag to Dini — a partial drain may be needed and hosts don't drain pools.
pH
Measures water acidity on a 0–14 scale. Controls how effectively chlorine sanitizes — at the right pH it's highly active; too high or low and it drops sharply. Affects surface and skin comfort too.

High (> 7.5): add muriatic acid per dosing chart. Circulate 10 min, re-test.

The water's real-time ability to destroy contaminants. A more direct indicator of sanitation strength than chlorine count alone. The IPS M920 reads this continuously and doses automatically — hosts recalibrate the controller if the Taylor reading and IPS display diverge.

Measures the water's ability to resist pH swings. pH's shock absorber — without enough TA, any chemical addition or heavy bather load sends pH swinging.

Dissolved calcium concentration. Too little and water pulls calcium from surfaces, etching plaster and tile. Too much and calcium deposits as cloudy scale. Updated weekly from Lucid's Monday readings and used in the LSI calculation.

Total Dissolved Solids — cumulative build-up of minerals, chemicals, and organics. As TDS climbs, sanitizer efficiency drops and water clarity suffers. Updated weekly from Lucid's Monday readings and used in the LSI calculation.

Nothing in pool chemistry changes just one thing. Before adding any chemical, know what else it moves — and check those parameters after.

HasachlorLiquid chlorine 12.5% (sodium hypochlorite) · adds FC directly FC ↑↑pH ↑ slight — Nudges pH up at higher doses. Re-check pH after a large addition. Do not add if pH is already high.
Sodium BicarbonateBaking soda · for low TA TA ↑↑pH ↑ — You cannot raise TA without also raising pH. If pH is already at or above 7.5, hold off or be prepared to monitor pH closely after.

Common Issues & What to Watch For
📡 IPS Controller Calibration Alert

The IPS controller readings drift over time — this is expected behavior, not a failure. That's why we recalibrate regularly. If the Taylor test result differs from the IPS display, the Taylor reading is the ground truth.

For pH: always compare your Taylor reading to the IPS controller display. If they differ, recalibrate the controller to match the Taylor result before making any chemical adjustments.

What to do: If the IPS display and Taylor reading consistently disagree on FC or pH, flag to Dini — the controller may need servicing.
🧪 Free Chlorine vs. Combined Chlorine Watch

Free chlorine (FC) is the active form that sanitizes. Combined chlorine (CC) is "spent" FC — it has already reacted with contaminants and provides zero sanitization, but it still shows up in ORP readings.

High CC means your water is working harder than the numbers suggest. It can also cause the chlorine smell guests sometimes notice — that's chloramines, not excess chlorine.

What to do: Any CC reading above 0 should be flagged to Dini. High CC typically indicates the need for shocking, which Dini and Lucid will manage.
🌡 pH Creep in Hot Water Watch

At 104°F, CO₂ naturally outgasses from the water, which pushes pH up over time. This is a structural tendency in hot soaks — you will fight pH creep consistently.

As pH climbs, active chlorine drops (see the chart above). A pool can look "in range" on FC but be under-sanitized simply because pH drifted high during a busy shift.

What to do: If pH is trending high across consecutive readings, alert Dini. Small, careful muriatic acid additions per dosing chart can bring it back. Re-check the IPS controller display after other chemicals have circulated before adding acid.
📋 Order of Operations Watch

This is the sequence Flux follows when adjusting water chemistry after testing:

  1. Check FC and pH together — both readings inform what comes next
  2. If FC is low: dose Hasachlor per the dosing chart
  3. If TA is low: add sodium bicarbonate per the dosing chart
  4. Circulate — run pumps to distribute the chemicals
  5. Re-test FC, pH, and TA
  6. If pH is now elevated (Hasachlor and bicarb both push it up): add muriatic acid per the dosing chart. Adjust pH once, at the end.

Do not adjust pH mid-session. Let the other chemicals circulate first, then correct pH at the end if needed.

Flag to Dini if more than one parameter is significantly out of range at once, especially if pH is already elevated before adding bicarb.
🧂 Sodium Bicarbonate Raising pH Watch

When you add baking soda to raise low TA, it will also raise pH. If pH is already at 7.5 or above, adding bicarb can push it out of range.

You can't raise TA without also affecting pH — plan for it every time.

What to do: Before adding bicarb, note where pH is sitting. If it's already at the high end of range, alert Dini before proceeding — a combined correction strategy may be needed.
☁️ CaCl₂ Turbidity (Clouding) Alert

Calcium chloride must be fully pre-dissolved before adding to the pool. If added dry or too quickly, calcium can precipitate immediately, causing white cloudiness that is difficult to reverse.

When the tool flags low CH, hosts add CaCl₂ on-site — technique is critical every time.

What to do: Always pre-dissolve fully in a bucket of pool water. Add slowly with pumps running. If clouding occurs after any addition, stop and alert Dini immediately.
